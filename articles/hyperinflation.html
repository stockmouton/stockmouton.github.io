TLDR

A quick overview of the game

Before diving into what is inflation and how it impacts investors, let’s look at the game itself.

We are playing the investment game. In this game you have investors on one side and assets on the other. Investors try to make money by investing into those assets and assets try to attract investors money to grow. This game is played all around the world, 24h/7: it’s a global economic competition and collaboration system.

investors

Investors plan is simple:

  • Invest money in assets
  • Wait
  • Profit (get back more money)

The difficult part is in “picking the right asset”. Investors have a nearly infinite amout of possible investments in this world: Housing, startup, stock, bonds, derivatives, art, etc. And, access to all the investment all around the world is at the tip of their fingers in real-time.

So, anyone can be an investor. Al you need is the will to follow the simple plan. The good news is that you can be any kind of investors by controlling any of those 3 phases:

  • The amount of your investment (from $0.01 to $1 Trillion).
  • The amount of time you are ready to let money in the asset (from 1 millisecond to a century).
  • The amount of risk you are ready to take (from risk-free assets to lottery ones).

As an investors all we have is our strategy and our model of the world. It’s a tough game because we can loose it all and, by the end of the day, we still need to live in the real world which has a cost: the amount of money we need to maintain our way of life. If we go as far as wanting to live from it or even better increase our wealth, the pressure to perform in this game starts to be aggressive.

We can measure our success with some simple math. As a rough first estimate, we can look at our return on investment (ROI):

\[ \text{ROI} = \frac{\text{Final value of the investment} - \text{Cost of the investment}}{\text{Cost of the investment}} \]

Our goal: maximise the ROI.

Multiple kinds of investors

Assets

On the other side, assets try to attract investors money. The need to satisfy any kind of investors spurs into reality any kind of assets: secure, risky, volatile, constant, expensive, cheap, etc. Any opportunity void will soon be filled.

To attract investors, assets are designed to make a promise of a nice return to investors. Sometimes, the promise is strongly grounded into financial mechanics, sometimes it’s just a pure promise, no ties atttached.

Their prices in the open market is mainly driven by supply and demand. But it’a not the only force that influence the market, an other important one is inflation.

What is inflation?

Inflation (and deflation) are words which refer to the rate of change in the price of “things”. Understand “things” as very broadly here: food, energy, house, stocks, bonds, ETF, etc. Anything that has a price.

To get the inflation rate:

  • you look at the current price of things now: \(p_t\).
  • You wait some time \(\Delta t\).
  • You look again at those same prices \(p_{t + \Delta t}\) and you calculate the ratio:

\[\text{inflation rate} = R_{inf} = \frac{p_{t + \Delta t}}{p_t}\]

  • If you have \(R_{inf} \gt 1\) we use the term inflation.
  • If you have \(R_{inf} \lt 1\), we use the term deflation.

Nothing more, nothing less.

Sidenote: notice how it is similar to the investor simple plan

The inflation rate can be measured for anything and people indeed measure the inflatin of everything. One inflation rate attract more attention than the others: the inflation rate measured by the Consumer price index (CPI) https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_price_index. And people tend to focus on it because it is supposed to represent the cost of life for lower and middle class (can be debated).

The problem is that this index is an average of many part and the government can influence some of them quite directly which could hide actual inflation.

Inflation is something that should be happening but at a moderate rate. As long as inflation fluctuates slowly, the economy can adapt smoothly and it should be fine. Problems arise when quick change happens (like hyperinflation or hyperdeflation). Near-zero inflation or deflation is considered harmfull too but for ther reasons (signs of bad economy).

Impact on investors

Inflation impact directly investors because if things starts to be more expensive, they need to earn even more money to keep what we call their “purchasing power”. The purchasing power is litteraly the power to buy things. If a $100 this year allows you to buy less things than a $100 5 years ago, then you lost some purchasing power. Since inflation rate measure how prices change, it becomes the direct measure of how much investors need to grow their amount of money to keep their purchasing power:

\[ \text{ROI} > \text{inflation rate} \]

When people take into account inflation to compute numbers, they add the word “real”. For example the real ROI is:

\[ \text{real ROI} = \text{ROI} - \text{inflation rate}\]

sidenote: The actual investors benefit per year must be computed by taking into account other costs like taxes and the cost of life during that year

On a given period of the past, it is easy to compute the real ROI. But things start to be more complicated when we talk about the future. If you want to anticipate what will be your real ROI you now have two numbers to guess: your expected ROI and the expected inflation during that same period.

In this predictions realm, people like to confirm their bias (psychological trait shared by the human specie) so be wary of any humans being too sure about its own prediction. It’s even harder as the open amrket is considered a second order chaotic system and by definition is unpredictable.

So we are stuck with statistics (oriented in the past), gut feeling, beliefs, reactory behaviours, etc. But those beliefs, when shared by many are a strong force that also impact the open market because they create expectations. We don’t know how many people that share an expectation is needed to influence the market but when it happens, the expectatin becomes a self-fuffiling prophecy: If enough people wants an asset to go up or down, it might very well happen.

Why I’m talking about that? Because expected inflation in particular is a very powerful force in the market and especially in the bond market.

We are going to dive into the basics of the bond and stock market to understand how those forces interacts with each other. we leave out derivatives (options, futures, etc.) and any other markets to keep it simple but we keep looking at the world markets.

Bonds and Stocks

Bonds and stocks represent two very different way to invest. Bonds are loans and stocks are pure investment. The main difference is that for bonds, you have a contract dictating how much return you will make when you buy it, and for stocks, you are mainly buying a promise that the company will grow and make more money.

Stocks

When you invest into stocks, you buy shares. It means you own a part of the company. The overall value of the company is represented by those share price and the amount of them. If the company makes a profit, it’s good to have those shares because you will be paid some money owning them(dividend). Those shares will also become more attractive and by the supply/demand effect, the share price will increase.

Those dividends are not certain, you might get any for many years (apple, amazon, etc.) but if the company is still growing anyway. You might not care as your goal could be to sell the shares you bought a low price, at high price.

Attractiveness of stocks drives supply/demands and they all compete for investors money. Investors have to make their own mind about how a stock is attractive or not and this point of view can widly differ.

Bonds

When you invest in bonds, things are very different.

First, bonds can not only be issued from corporations but also from municipals, states and governements. Second, a bond is issued with the following properties:

  • Face value: the price at which the bond is issued
  • The coupon rate (interest rate): Percentage per year of the face value paid to the bond holder. Can be fixed or variable.
  • Maturity date: the date at which the amount of borrowed money (Call the principal = the face value) is given back to the bond holder.

Bonds are interesting because they lower the risk of losing money.The usual flow for bonds is as follow:

  • You pay the face value to buy the bonds when they are issued
  • You receive a fixed-income equal to the coupon rate every year.
  • You get back your money at mthe maturity date.

Good, it reduces uncertainty but in exchange, bonds provide less potential ROI than stocks.

Since bonds are predictable, you can easily compare them with the current economic situation. To be attractive, the coupon rate must be important enough relative to teh economic situation and in particular, the inflation rate. But since bonds have a maturity date that can go be very long, people are way of inflation variation in the future. This is why the expected inflation rate matters some much.

Investors won’t buy bonds if they think their interest rate is lower than what they expect inflation to be over the bonds validity period.

On the other side, the one issuing bonds is borrowing money and would rather borrow at low interest. Those opposite incentives control supply and demands in the bond market.

What happens after bonds are issued?

The story does not stop here. If you buy a bond when it is issued, you are not stuck with it until the maturity date. The bond market is here so you can trade bonds after. At that point, bonds are competing with other bonds and more generally with other assets.

Even though the face value of the bond has been fixed when it was emitted, it’s current price can fluctuate. This leads to a new notion: the yield.

The yield isSince the coupon’s value has been calculated relatively to the face value of the bond. This one is fixed though time. The bond yield is the dynamic interest rate and is calculated like that:

\[ yield = \frac{\text{annual coupon payment}}{\text{current bond price}}\]

As a quick example, a bond with a face value of \(1000 with a fixed interest rate of 5% lead to a total coupon of 50\) per year. if the price of the bonds decrease to 900$ the yield is now \(\frac{50}{900} = 0.55 = 55%\) The yield is by definition, inversely linked to the bond price: the the yield increase, the bond price decrease and vice-versa.

Bonds yields are a good measure for investors because you can compare it directly to the inflation rate or your expected inflation rate for the period of time you want to hold onto it. Outside of special circumstance or external intervention bonds price should move as to satisify the following:

\[ \text{yield} > \text{inflation rate} \Rightarrow \text{current price} < \frac{\text{coupon's value}}{\text{inflation rate}}\]

How to perceive bonds risk?

So far we observed only one risk on bond price: the evolution of the inflation rate.

But there is a second one: the risk that the borrower do not return the money at the maturity date or even can’t pay the interest. We say that the bond default. It’s rare to see a bond default before maturity date, because the coupon’s value should not be very high. At maturity, the total principal is huge if they borrower as not grow enough, it might in trouble to reimburse the full amount. In most case, and when the economy is all right, the borrower will just restructure, or issue new bonds and everything is fine. But the risk still exists.

To mitigate that risk, people came together ans created rating agencies. Their goal is to rate bonds on their default risk. There is quite a lot of different grades and those grades are different between agencies but ultimately, the result is that bonds end up to be either “investment grade” or “junk bonds”.

The distinction has a big impact on bonds price because naturally, investors will want higher interest rate for higher risk. If a company starts to struggle financially, it’s bond might be downgraded by rating agencies and if they happen to fall under the junk bonds umbrella, the market will react by asking a higher yield and so, the bond price will fall. This is especially bad for those so-called zombies company. Zombies company are the one that survive only thanks to their debt and make barely enough money to reimburse the coupon. Those companies are in real trouble if rates increase and so are very risky.

Hyperinflation

Hyperinflation is a sudden and strong inflation rate increase. Concretely, if it happens in 24h and it amount to 50%, it would mean that the baguette you were buying at 1$ so far, cost now 1.5$! It would be quite f**ed up. It’s never that quick but even if it takes a year it would be extrememly difficult for many people to keep a decent life.

Impact on the open market

As we’ve already seen, bonds yield (or interest rate at issuance) must be higher than inflation to attract investors. But if the inflation rate increase rapidly, what you quickly see will be a demand for higher yields bonds and so existing bonds price will crash and stabilize at a new equilibrium satysfying the new demand for higher yields.

But the impact does not stop here.

First, investors that have high inflation expectation will start to sell bonds before the price decrease adding more fuel to fire. Second, companies that need to borrow money might not be able to do so because interest rates for new bonds must be high too which means that you will have to pay an expensive price regularly for your debt. Third, existing companies that have variable interest rate might suffer directly because they might have to pay more per year. Fourth, existing companies that already borrow money using bonds and are getting closer to their maturity date might not be able to reimburse. When interet rates satys the same though a long period of time, it is no problem as the company will issue new bonds, to reimburse the principals of the old one. But now that interest rised greatly, it might be impossible for it and they might default their bonds.

All those effects increae risk in the bond market, and investors will ask for even higher yields.

And it doesn’t stop here because after the bond market crash, now investors can find high yields in the bond market. And investors will start to rotate from other assets to bonds. This usually have a big impact on the stock market as a lot of money will come from it and so you should see some part of the stock market to crash. Especially bubbles.

Sidenote: usually the bond market crash and stock market are synchronised because the open market is full of robot and reactionnary investors.

Remember that we are only looking at investors point of view, but actually this will be very bad for the economy overall. Investment will slow down, economic growth will not recover directly you end up in a recession period which is a correction for the real economy. I’m not even talking about the struggles the lower class would face abd the fact that you might want to increase minimum wage which would increase demands and potentially increae even more the inflation rate. There are lots of twisted feedback loops regarding inflation.

For the economy and the market, the goal is to get back to a new equilibrium quickly, or even better, if we have to go down at least control this downward movement to avoid drastic changes in people life.

Central banks tooling

How central banks actually print money? In the pase, they atually printed money for real but nowadays, it’s mainly done through the banking system and the open market. Outside of directly controlling the money supply, cental banks can also change the current economic incentives. They do so by setting interest rates that impact the whole economy. And again, they can officialy or not do so. Let’s see what are those interest rates and how.

In the US, the most important interest rates (called key rates) are the federal fund rate and the discount rate:

  • The federal fund rate is is the amount of interest central private banks charge each-other when lending their excess reserves. This impact all loans across the board as banks will increase their interest rates depending on this one (which impact the cost of credits for borrower).
  • The discount rate is the amount of interest central banks charge private banks for short-term loans. When short-term loans can’t borrow money from other private banks, they start to ask their central banks. Acting on it, will either incentivize banks to borrow from the central bank or not. This impact the global money supply on the market as central banks are the only creting/destroying money.

Before enacting any change in key rates or using any of the following tool (called open market operations, OMO), the FED (and FOMC) analyse the eonomy and do a speech. In those speech, they will mention any rate change, open market operations but they will also provide their forecasr which investors listen closely. It is also interesting to listen the next few days on the market reaction, usually people will start to communicate and what they think about hte current situation.

  • Yield curve control (YCC, continuous control) The FED targets a long term interest rate by buying and/or selling treasury bonds, the number of bonds needed is unccaped, the goal is to control their to keep their yield at the targeted long term interest rate. The central bank will keep buying/selling treasury bonds for any amount if time it fells needed. The treasury-bond is then completely controled by the central bank.

  • Quantitative Easing (QE, instantaneous injection) The FED buys a massive, but specific, amount of bonds, reducing the supply of those bonds on the bond market, which increase their value and so decrease their yield. This is a good way to reduce yield for treasury bonds while augmenting the money supply on the market. The goal here is to inject liquidity at a given time, not target a specific interest rate: after the move, the market come back to its usual dynamic. Also, any kind of bond can be bought by the central bank, it is not targeted at treasury bonds.

  • Helicopter money (people QE, instantaneous injection) What is called helicopter money is jsut a direct deposit in people bank accounts. It’s a form of quantitative easing but directed at the population directly. It is known to increase demand and inflation more directly.

  • Stimulus This is just an umbrella term that is mainly used by the governement to package a set of bills together. It can contains any mix of preivous tools and tas adjustment.

Central banks have other tools but the prupose of this post is to not to give a complete overview. The previously cited tools are the one with the strongest impact on the open market which is our main subject.

Flywheels

Instead of spending time writing a paragraph, let me just pus a flywheel to you:

  • Inflation rate increase: price of things increase -> Cost of existing debt decrease (good for existing borrower) -> Comodities price increase (commodities stocks increase) -> Real estate price increase (real estate stocks increase) -> Companies selling things make more money -> FED decide to increase federal fund rate and discount rate to follow the inflation rate -> interbank loan rate increase -> everyone loan and credit rate increase -> cost of borrow increase (not good for new borrower) -> borrowing in general slows down -> Companies might not be able to borrow anymore (or issue new bonds) -> zombie companies might struggle to pay back their principals (can’t roll-over existing debt) -> junk-bonds default increase -> Investors try to rotate out of junk bonds before it happens -> other assets value increase -> Growth companies functionning on debt might struggle to borrow -> Rating agency might decrease their grade -> Some companies moves from “investment-grade” to “junk” bonds (fallen angel) -> Drastic bond yield increase -> at the beginning (or before) short-term investor rotates out the bond market to avoid bonds price decrease -> other assets value increase -> at the end (when yield stabilize at a new level), short term investors rotate back in to gather high yield bonds -> other assets value decrease -> bonds treasury yield increase -> bonds market decrease and so bond yields increase -> long term investors (pension funds) rotate internally of the bond market gather those high yields bonds
  • Inflation rate increase: price of things increase -> FED do not like it and use QE to increase generic bond price and/or YCC to ensure treasury yields are constant and/or keep key rates low -> increase inflation in the open market (moneh injection) -> The money dump should increase other assets value (including the stock market) -> real treasury yield starts to be negative -> Long term bond investors (pension fund) might not like it and rotate out of the bond market -> A fight between the FED and other bond investors start to kick in (FED wants to keep low yield, bonds investors wnat to increase yield) -> depending on the winner, more or less bond investors rotate in or out (2008 crisis)

  • Reopening the economy (supply has decrease) + stimulus with direct payment -> governement increase debt -> rating agency could lower the US grade -> treasury yield increase -> Expected inflation rate increase -> Money reach everyone -> spending increase -> demand increase -> inflation increase

  • Expected inflation rate increase -> The market might preempt increase in bond yields -> investors rotate out of the bond market -> bond yields increase (self-fulfilling) -> other assets value (including stock market) increase -> FED announce QE or YCC -> reverse the dynamic

  • Hyperinflation happens: drastic price increase -> the open market enters into short-term reactionnary behaviour -> Bond market crash -> Growth stock crash -> Commodities explode
