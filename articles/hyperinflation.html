Demistification

WTF is hyperinflation? Hyperinflation is a sudden change of the inflation rate.

Thanks dummy, WTF is this inflation rate then? Inflation (and deflation) are two words to refer to the rate of change in the price of “things”. Understand “things” as very broadly here: food, energy, house, stocks, bonds, ETF, etc. Anything that is sold.

What people do is that they look at the current price of things now (p_t) then they look at the price of those things 2 month later (p_t+!) and calculate the ratio: \(inflation rate = \frac{p_{t+1}}{p_t}\). If this ratio is > 1 we use the term inflation, if it’s lower, we use the term deflation.

So hyperinflation, is the situation when a sudden and strong change happens. Like you went to buy your baguette yesterday at 1$ and today the baker ask 1.5$ (ho my). In this example the inflation rate would be 50% over one day. (it’s a rate because you need two point in time to get it).

So you can measure the inflation rate of anything but the one that people usually refers to is the inflation rate measured by the Consumer price index (CPI) https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_price_index.

What are investors (we)?

  • An investor is anyone who is willing to give money to someone in the hope that this someone will give more momey back later. That’s it. Nothing more, nothing less. Even if you have only 1$, you can be an investor.

The main goal of investors is to make money, the more they can make the happier they are. The second goal of investors is to minimize risks. They want to make money but they don’t want to take the risk to lose money. And since every investment contains some risk, a good investors is the one balancing risk and reward successfully.

This lead our usual investor to follow those simple rules: - For a fixed risk, one would invest into the opportunity providing more return on investment (ROI) - For a fixed ROI, one would invest in the less risky one.

From a investor point of view, anything can now be compared with those 2 properties: risk and return, we can rename the return as a “rate” as long as we divide by the duration of the investment.

So we now focus on those 2 properties: risk and rate. The goal is to minimize risk while maximising rate i.e. maximising the amount of money you are making in the lowest possible amount of time.

This is how you can compare anything even investors themselves. Sould you invest in stock, bonds, ETF, funds, …? Let’s compare the rate on a given period for those investment!

That said, let me note that the lowest rate you should aim for is not 0% but the inflation rate. If you go under the inflation rate, you effectively lost money during that period

What are bonds?

https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bond.asp https://www.thestreet.com/investing/frequently-asked-questions-about-the-bond-market-1087488 https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/09/difference-between-yields-and-interest-rate.asp

When you need to make a loan, you usually go see the bank but what if you need more money that one bank can provide? This is when you use bonds. Each bond is a tiny loan, like a tiny contract, containing the following properties: - The original price of the bond (before it reaches the market) - The coupon, this is the fixed income part. - the interval at which the coupon is paid - Finally the date at which the bond mature, meaning at which the full loan will be reimbursed.

the difference with usual loans is that bonds end up on the open market just like stocks. So you can resell your bond to someone else and as usual, the bond price fluctuate because of supply and demand. The trick to understand is that it necessrily impact the yield of that bond.

Inflation rate not only impact how much you pay things, but it also impact the stock market which impaact the bank which imapct for a second time, you.

How is that? It has to do with bonds.

Bonds are loan, but in the stock market. When you buy a bonds, you simply agree on a ROI at a given date. as a simple example the us governement as the FED might issue bonds with the following properties: - cost 100\(, ROI 3%, 1year It means that if you buty one bond at 100\) from the FED, you are sure to get back 103$ in one year.

Those bonds from the FED are special because they are what we call “risk free”. Unless the USA disappears, you will indeed get your 103$ in one year. And because those are risk free, any other risk-free investment will have to match that rate because if not, investors will just move their money away from less interesting bonds.

To make that more official, the FED actually defines every two month the FED target interest rate. This is the rate at which following bonds will be issued. It is a “target” because it is not enforced by the govrnement but supply/demand and the market should quickly react to match it usually.

This rate actually impact you, because it impacts how all loans rate in the US computed. Even those small loans on your credit card.

FED rate -> bonds rate -> bank rate. If that rate is high, you might be tempted as an investors to invest into this risk free product: you might rotate your investment from stocks to bonds -> stocks go down.

But if IR is upper that the target rate, then the opposite will happens, investors will move away from bonds and go into something else like stocks.

How to control inflation?

The FED as multiple tools to do so and it is all about debt

  • Yield curve control (continuous control) The FED targets a long term interest rate by buying and/or selling bonds, the number of bonds needed is unccaped, the goal is to keep their price at a given target to have their interest rate at the long term interest rate. It will keep buying/selling to maintains bonds interest rate at that given target for a “long time”. The bond market is then completely controled by the FED https://www.investopedia.com/what-is-yield-curve-control-4797189

  • Quantitative easing (instantaneous injection) The FED buys a massive, but specific, amount of bonds, reducing the supply of those bonds on the bond market, which increase their value and so decrease their interest rate. At the same time, the amount of money on market increase which makes it more liquid. The goal here is to inject liquidity at a given time, not target a specific interest rate: after the move, the market come back to its usual dynamic. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_easing

  • Interest on excessive reserves (defined by the FOMC, subgroup of the FED) which gives an incentives to bank to keep an excess reserve (pulling money off the economy)
